How to read this book

The book has six parts. They are presented in the order an enterprise team usually encounters them — delivery first, then platform, then agents, then the ML/DL fundamentals that power them. If you are a new learner, you almost certainly want to read the parts in a different order. Pick the path that matches your role:

🌱 New to ML or just learning the field

  1. Part IV — ML Foundations (PyTorch, regression, classification, trees)
  2. Part V — Deep Learning & LLMs in order: FFN → CNN → RNN → Transformers → fine‑tuning → alignment
  3. Part III — Agent Protocols once you understand what an LLM is
  4. Part II and Part I when you start shipping

🔧 Backend / platform engineer learning AI

  1. Part II chapters 1–5 (FastAPI, concurrency, background work) — already familiar territory
  2. Part II chapters 6–15 for the auth + identity + multi‑tenancy stack
  3. Part III end‑to‑end (MCP → A2A → LangChain)
  4. Spot‑read Transformers (Part V Ch. 11), fine‑tuning (Ch. 12), and alignment (Ch. 13)
  5. Part I when you take your first AI project to production

📑 Lead / Principal AI engineer or architect

  1. Part I end‑to‑end (delivery lifecycle)
  2. Part II Ch. 6–15 for the auth/identity story you'll review
  3. Part III for protocol‑level vocabulary in design reviews
  4. Transformers, fine‑tuning, alignment as conceptual reference
  5. Appendix A for a 60‑minute narrative covering all of the above

📞 Preparing for a Lead DS / Lead AI interview

  1. Appendix A — Lead DS interview talk track (start here)
  2. Drill the delivery lifecycle and auth deep‑dive
  3. Be able to whiteboard attention and explain the difference between SFT/PEFT/DPO and ORPO/GRPO/KTO

Reading the book front‑to‑back is also valid — it tells the story from the business side inward. Just be aware that Part III assumes Part II Ch. 1–3, and Part V assumes Part IV.
