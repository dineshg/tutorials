From Pain Point → Lead AI Engineer

This is a practical, beginner-friendly guide to how an AI use case typically originates and travels through a large enterprise (e.g., a major bank or a national airline) until it lands with a Lead AI Engineer. It also explains common roles: EA, Ops, EM, GM, and Business Units (BUs).

1) Big picture

In large enterprises, AI work rarely begins with “let’s build an LLM.” It begins with: a business pain → a formal initiative → governance and triage → delivery ownership.

Key idea: The “AI lead” is usually brought in once the problem needs cross-system design (identity, data access, retrieval, model, audit), not when someone has a vague idea.

2) Roles glossary (who is who)

Titles vary by company; these are typical meanings in Australia-based enterprises.

Role What they do Why they matter to the AI project
EA (Executive Assistant) Supports an executive: calendars, meetings, packs, minutes, action items, briefings. Often the “workflow owner” for meeting artifacts and “what was decided.” EAs are often power users who can define the top recurring questions and what “proof” looks like (citations, links, timestamps). They validate whether the tool saves real time.
Ops (Operations) “Run the business day-to-day.” In airlines, this can include operations control/disruption recovery. In banks, business ops, service ops, incident/change, compliance operations, etc. Ops stakeholders define urgency, failure tolerance, and what a “wrong answer” costs. They will demand reliability, speed, and auditability.
SME (Subject Matter Expert) Domain expert in policy/process/operations. Knows the right sources and interpretations. Provides ground truth for evaluation sets, validation of answers, and “what’s allowed” guidance.
Business Owner Accountable for business outcomes: scope, priorities, success metrics, adoption. Often the person who “accepts” the product for business use. Without a business owner, pilots often become demos with no adoption. This person drives change management.
Sponsor Senior executive champion who removes blockers and provides authority (and often budget). Helps secure access approvals, resolves conflicts, and unlocks resources.
EM (Engineering Manager) Manages engineering team delivery: staffing, planning, quality, execution, career support. Partner to the AI lead: ensures team capacity, delivery cadence, and operational readiness.
GM (General Manager) Senior leader for a function or business line (e.g., GM Digital, GM Operations, GM Data/AI). Owns strategy, budget, and high-level outcomes. Often the decision-maker who approves the initiative and sets constraints (risk appetite, pilot group, timelines).
Security / IAM Defines identity, access control, least privilege, secrets handling, network requirements. AI projects fail without secure-by-design access to data sources and proper audit controls.
Privacy / Legal / Risk Ensures compliance obligations are met (PII, retention, audit, customer data restrictions). Defines what data the model can see and under what controls; reviews release readiness.
Data Owner Accountable for a dataset/document corpus (approves access, usage, retention). Approves what documents you can ingest and how you classify/store them.
Lead AI Engineer Designs and delivers production AI systems end-to-end: data → retrieval → model → evaluation → ops. Converts vague ideas into an executable plan with guardrails, metrics, and reliable delivery.

3) What is a BU (Business Unit) and why does it matter?

A Business Unit (BU) is a major organisational division that owns a slice of the business: its goals, processes, teams, data, and budgets.

Examples (varies by company):

  • Airline BU examples: Network Ops, Customer Experience, Digital, Loyalty, Revenue Management
  • Bank BU examples: Retail Banking, Business Banking, Wealth, Risk, Technology, Operations

Why BUs matter for your tool:

  • BUs often have different data owners and different risk appetite.
  • They may require different access boundaries (who can see what documents).
  • Funding and sponsorship often comes from a specific BU.
Practical: Many initiatives start in one BU (pilot), then expand cross-BU if proven safe and valuable.

4) The typical flow: pain point → AI lead

This is the “enterprise conveyor belt” a use case rides before it becomes a real build.

Pain felt in BU → Quantified by EA/Ops/SME → BU manager/GM sponsorship → Brief written → AI/Architecture intake gate → Security/Privacy triage → Routed to AI delivery team → Lead AI Engineer owns plan

Step 1 — Pain point appears inside a BU

It shows up as a workflow problem, not “AI”:

  • Time loss: execs/EAs searching meeting notes/packs/emails
  • Decision friction: hard to reconstruct “who said what and when”
  • Operational risk: staff can’t find latest approved guidance during incidents
  • Customer impact: frontline teams can’t find policy answers quickly

The BU feels it because it directly impacts throughput and outcomes.

Step 2 — Someone makes it real (EA/Ops/Compliance)

This is the trigger point. Typical voices:

  • EA: “We spend 5–10 hours/week per exec chasing meeting artifacts and compiling answers.”
  • Ops: “During disruptions/incidents, information retrieval is the bottleneck.”
  • Risk/Compliance: “We spend days reconstructing evidence and decision trails.”
Why EAs matter: they live inside the executive workflow and define what “useful output” looks like.
Why Ops matters: they define urgency and what “wrong answer” costs.

Step 3 — The BU converts pain into an initiative

The BU manager or GM (often with a sponsor) decides: “this is worth pursuing.” It becomes:

  • a roadmap item (internal product)
  • a transformation initiative (knowledge management / AI uplift)
  • a risk/governance program (auditability)
  • an ops improvement effort (incident response)

A short brief is drafted: problem, users, constraints, desired outcome, rough ROI.

Step 4 — AI intake / architecture / governance triage

Enterprises run gate checks before they invest:

  • Is it actually AI, or should we fix search/metadata/source systems first?
  • Do we have data access rights and clear data ownership?
  • What is the sensitivity classification (PII, strategy, HR)?
  • What’s the failure risk (wrong answers) and what controls are required?
  • Can success be measured?
Common outcome: “Pilot only” with a tightly approved corpus and strict access controls.

Step 5 — Work is routed to delivery (AI lead gets it)

Now it lands as an actionable request, usually with constraints like:

  • “We need citations and audit logs.”
  • “Access must be restricted by user / business unit / group membership.”
  • “We must meet security/privacy controls.”

The AI lead gets it because it spans identity, ingestion, retrieval, model behavior, evaluation, and operations.

5) Concrete example (meeting-note Q&A)

Problem: Exec asks “What did Chandima mention about no meeting on 1 Dec?”

Current state: EA manually searches packs/minutes/emails and compiles a reply.

Desired state: tool returns:

  • short answer
  • citation: exact snippet from the 1 Dec meeting note + link
  • safe behavior: if not found, say so and suggest next best query
EA success signal: “I can copy/paste this to the exec and it has evidence.”
Ops success signal: “It’s fast, reliable, and never guesses when evidence is missing.”

6) What arrives on the AI lead’s desk (typical package)

  • Brief: the business problem, users, constraints
  • Sponsor: a GM or senior leader backing it
  • Business owner: accountable for adoption and success metrics
  • Data owners: who approves the corpus (meeting notes/packs)
  • Security/privacy constraints: what must be enforced

At this stage, the AI lead is expected to turn this into: scope, architecture, pilot plan, eval plan, and operating model.

7) What the Lead AI Engineer does on day 1

Day 1 outputs (what “lead-level” looks like)

  • Problem reframing: “Cited Q&A over approved meeting corpus, scoped by identity.”
  • MVP slice: one BU/corpus + top 1–2 question types
  • Access model: who can see what (group gating + document corpus allowlist)
  • Success metrics: citation correctness, groundedness, time saved
  • Evaluation plan: a “golden set” of questions + release gates
  • Delivery plan: team lanes + dependencies + timeline
Key lead move: co-design with EAs and Ops early. They define workflow reality and failure tolerance.
