Architecture Review Tutorial (Step 2): Run the Review + Capture Approval Conditions

This tutorial shows how to run an enterprise architecture review for the Exec Document Q&A pilot and how to walk out with a clear approval outcome and actionable conditions. It is written for an AI Lead who must align Security, Platform, Data Owners, and Engineering.

1) Goal and outcomes

Goal Get Approved to build pilot (or Approved with conditions) and leave with explicit controls to satisfy.

You want to leave with
  • Approval outcome: approved / approved with conditions / rejected
  • Action items with owners + due dates
  • Required controls: redaction rules, log schema, data classification constraints, etc.
  • Decision log: what was decided and why
What “success” looks like
  • Reviewers agree the trust boundary is safe (LLM data minimised)
  • Access control is clear: org allowlist + tenant/corpus gates
  • Audit and observability are adequate for production governance
  • Pilot scope is bounded and safe
Lead-level framing: the review is not about “is LLM cool?”. It’s about risk-managed delivery: identity, data boundaries, auditability, and operability.

2) Who attends (typical enterprise roles)

Adjust to your org, but a good minimum set is:

  • Architecture/Platform (GKE, networking, landing zone patterns)
  • Security/IAM (identity, group gating, service accounts, secrets)
  • Privacy/Risk (data classification, retention, external model boundary)
  • Data Owner / Business Owner (corpus approval, acceptable use)
  • Engineering Manager (delivery plan, support model, runbooks)
  • AI Lead (you) and optionally AI Head (exec sponsor / escalation)
Tip: If your AI Head is not present, confirm they support the risk posture and pilot scope beforehand. The review goes smoother when leadership alignment is obvious.

3) Preparation checklist (the difference between “approval” and “rework”)

Objective Remove surprises before the meeting.

  • Share the pack 48–72 hours prior.
  • Do 1:1 pre-reads with Security and Platform (15–30 mins each) to surface blockers early.
  • Confirm data classification allowed for pilot corpus (even a “pilot-only” constraint is fine).
  • Confirm “what goes to the LLM” is explicitly stated and minimised.
  • Bring your draft go-live gates (audit + eval + leakage tests) to show operational maturity.
Common failure mode: reviewers first learn that “documents might be sent to an external model” during the meeting. Always state boundaries upfront.

4) What to bring (minimum review pack)

The meeting works best when all inputs are concise and anchored on risk controls. Here is the practical minimum:

Bring What it is Why reviewers care Minimum content
HLD High-level architecture + service choices Trust boundaries, data flows, major services Diagrams, service selection (with alternatives), identity model, data flow
ADRs Decision records (“why we chose X”) Prevents re-litigating decisions later Tenant isolation, cited-only answers, model boundary, retrieval choice, eval gates
Threat model summary Top risks + mitigations Security & privacy posture Cross-tenant leakage, injection, exfiltration, logging/PII, access abuse
Draft go-live gates Release criteria Operational readiness & governance Audit logging fields, evaluation thresholds, leakage tests, rollback plan
Shortcut: If time is tight, prioritise (1) trust boundary, (2) identity/access enforcement, (3) audit/evals.

5) Recommended review agenda (60 minutes)

  1. 5 min — Purpose and decision asks (what approvals you need today)
  2. 10 min — Architecture overview (diagrams + trust boundaries)
  3. 10 min — Identity & access (Workspace OIDC, org allowlist, tenant/corpus isolation)
  4. 10 min — Data flows & model boundary (what is stored, what is sent to LLM, what is logged)
  5. 10 min — Threat model top risks (and mitigations)
  6. 10 min — Go-live gates (audit, evals, leakage tests, runbooks)
  7. 5 min — Decisions + conditions (capture outcome + owners)
Good review behavior: move quickly from “what it is” to “how we prevent bad outcomes”.

6) Questions reviewers will ask (be ready)

Security / IAM
  • How do you validate tokens and gate access?
  • Where is tenant/corpus enforced?
  • How do service accounts access Cloud SQL/GCS/Secret Manager?
  • What is logged? Do logs contain sensitive content?
Privacy / Risk
  • What data leaves GCP to the LLM provider?
  • What data classifications are allowed in the pilot?
  • What’s retention policy (docs, embeddings, logs)?
  • How do you prevent “prompt injection” from documents?
Platform / Architecture
  • Why GKE vs Cloud Run? What’s the platform standard?
  • How will this be operated (monitoring, alerts, runbooks)?
  • How do you handle dependency failures (retrieval/LLM down)?
Business / Data owners
  • Who owns the documents? Who approves inclusion/exclusion?
  • What’s the success metric for the pilot?
  • How do we prove answers are grounded/cited?

7) Capturing approval conditions (this is the most important output)

Enterprises often approve with conditions. Your job is to turn those conditions into trackable work with owners and evidence required.

Technique: re-state each condition in the meeting in plain language and confirm agreement: “So we’re approved provided we do X, Y, Z. Owner A, due date B. Evidence is C.”

Example conditions (common for GenAI)

  • Redaction policy for sensitive patterns before sending to LLM
  • Logging schema that excludes raw document content but captures citations + metadata
  • Data classification constraints for pilot corpus (“only internal restricted, no secret/regulated”)
  • Leakage tests that prove tenant/corpus filters work
  • Evaluation gates (accuracy/citation correctness thresholds)
  • Runbook + on-call ownership

Condition tracker template (copy/paste)

Condition Owner Due Evidence required Status
Define and implement redaction rules before model calls AI Lead YYYY-MM-DD PR + tests + example before/after Open
Audit log schema approved by Security (no raw snippets) Security + Eng YYYY-MM-DD Signed-off schema + sample logs Open
Leakage test suite (cross-tenant retrieval must return 0) Data/Eng YYYY-MM-DD CI test results + report Open
Anti-pattern: leaving with “Security wants some logging controls” without an owner and measurable evidence.

8) Post-review artifacts (what you send out)

  • Decision summary (1 page): outcome + approved scope + key decisions
  • Condition tracker: conditions, owners, dates, evidence
  • Action items list: meeting minutes with explicit assignments
  • Updated HLD/ADRs: incorporate agreed changes
  • Go-live gate checklist: what must be true before pilot launch
Operational tip: send the summary within 24 hours while the meeting context is fresh.

9) Templates you can copy (email + meeting close)

Meeting close script (30 seconds)

“To close: our outcome is [approved / approved with conditions / rejected]. Conditions are [A, B, C] with owners [X, Y, Z] and due dates [d1, d2, d3]. Evidence required is [e1, e2, e3]. I’ll circulate notes and the tracker today.”

Follow-up email skeleton

  • Approved scope: Pilot cohort + allowed corpora + allowed data classifications
  • Conditions: (link to tracker)
