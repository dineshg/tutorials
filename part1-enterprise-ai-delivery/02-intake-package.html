Enterprise Intake Package: What Arrives on the Lead AI Engineer’s Desk

This HTML tutorial is designed for future Lead AI Engineers in large enterprises (banks/airlines). It describes the typical “package” you receive when a business use case is ready to become a real AI delivery. The goal is to help you quickly assess readiness, risks, and your day-1 actions.

1) Why an “intake package” exists

In an enterprise, “AI requests” are filtered through governance (security/privacy/risk/architecture) so delivery teams don’t start with a vague idea. When the request reaches you, it should already have:

  • a defined business problem
  • an owner accountable for outcomes
  • approved or approvable data sources
  • constraints (privacy/security/audit) made explicit
Lead-level expectation: you convert the package into scope, architecture, evaluation gates, operating model, and a pilot plan — not “build a demo.”

2) What you should receive (the “minimum viable intake”)

If you don’t receive these, ask for them immediately. You can still proceed in discovery mode, but you should treat it as “not ready for build.”
Business brief
  • problem statement (“what hurts”)
  • users + workflow (who does what today)
  • example questions/tasks
  • success metrics (time saved, accuracy, adoption)
Ownership
  • Sponsor (senior leader who backs it)
  • Business owner (accountable for outcomes + adoption)
  • named representatives: EA + Ops + SME
Data scope
  • which sources (Drive/SharePoint/Confluence/email/transcripts)
  • who the data owner is for each source
  • what is in-scope vs out-of-scope
  • data sensitivity classification (PII/confidential/etc.)
Constraints & controls
  • access control expectations (“who can see what”)
  • audit/logging requirements
  • privacy requirements (PII handling, retention)
  • risk tolerance: “what happens if it’s wrong?”
Delivery expectations
  • pilot cohort size and timeline
  • support model (who owns it after launch)
  • non-functional requirements: latency, uptime
Approvals / gates
  • security/IAM contact for approvals
  • privacy/legal/risk contact if needed
  • architecture review process (if required)

3) Who the people are (and what you should expect from them)

Role What they provide in the intake What you should ask them
Sponsor (often GM-level) Authority, budget influence, unblock access issues, approve pilot scope “What’s the business outcome, and how will we measure success?”
Business owner Accountability for adoption, priorities, and acceptance criteria “Which workflows are must-have for pilot? What is the rollout plan?”
EA (Executive Assistant) Real workflow + top recurring exec questions + acceptable answer format “What does ‘proof’ look like? Link/snippet/timestamp? What’s the top 20 questions?”
Ops (Operations) Urgency constraints + failure tolerance + what “wrong answer” costs “When it’s wrong, what’s the impact? When it’s missing, how should it behave?”
SME (Subject Matter Expert) Ground truth interpretations + what docs matter + evaluation support “What sources are authoritative? What are common ambiguous cases?”
Data owner Approval for access, usage, retention, classification rules “What is allowed to be ingested? Any exclusions? How should retention work?”
Security/IAM Access model constraints, least privilege guidance, secrets/network expectations “What’s the approved pattern for identity, service accounts, and audit logging?”
Privacy/Legal/Risk PII/regulated-data constraints, release conditions, policy compliance “What data can leave the boundary? What redaction/logging rules apply?”
EM (Engineering Manager) Resourcing, delivery cadence, quality bar, operational ownership “What’s the team capacity and what must we trade off to hit pilot?”
Quick reminder: “EA” and “Ops” are not merely “stakeholders.” They often define the workflow reality and the acceptable output format. If you skip them, you risk building something that demos well but doesn’t get used.

4) Red flags (signals the intake is not ready)

  • No business owner: nobody accountable for adoption → high risk of “demo only.”
  • No data owner identified: access will stall later.
  • Unbounded scope: “search all documents in the company” on day 1 → push back to a pilot corpus.
  • No success metric: you can’t prove impact → define baseline and targets.
  • No risk statement: “wrong answers are fine” is rarely true in enterprise.
  • No access model: unclear whether users have different document permissions.
Hard stop pattern: If they want “free-form Q&A over everything” without citations, auditing, or access control, you should reframe to a safe pilot first.

5) What you do on day 1 (as Lead AI Engineer)

Your day-1 goal

Turn the intake package into a delivery-ready plan with measurable outcomes and safety controls.

Day-1 outputs (typical)

  1. Problem reframing into a single sentence that implies controls (e.g., “cited answers over approved corpus”).
  2. MVP definition: one BU, one corpus, 1–2 question patterns.
  3. Evaluation plan: a golden set of 30–50 questions + citation correctness rubric.
  4. Risk register: cross-user leakage, hallucinations, sensitive data exposure, adoption failure.
  5. Architecture sketch: ingestion → canonical store → retrieval → LLM → citations → audit log.
  6. Team plan: lanes (ingestion, backend/security, retrieval/LLM/eval) + owners + sprint goals.
Lead-level move: schedule a co-design session with EAs and Ops within week 1. Ask them for the top recurring questions and define “acceptable evidence.”
