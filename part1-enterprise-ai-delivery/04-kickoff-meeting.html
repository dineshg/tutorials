Pilot Kickoff Meeting (AI Lead)

This tutorial explains the kickoff meeting that happens right after the AI Lead submits the “AI Lead Response Doc”. The example is your Executive Document Q&A tool (meeting notes/packs → cited answers). The purpose of this kickoff is to turn the plan into committed decisions so delivery can start safely.

1) Why this kickoff meeting exists

In enterprise delivery, pilots fail when you start building before the organisation commits to: who is in the pilot, what data is approved, and how success will be measured.

Kickoff objective: leave the meeting with hard decisions so engineering does not stall on approvals later.

In plain English

  • The business wants a tool.
  • You (AI lead) want to build it safely.
  • Security/privacy/data owners want control.
  • This meeting aligns everyone and locks the minimum safe pilot.

2) Inputs you bring (so the meeting is productive)

Document 1

AI Lead Response Doc

Scope, safety, architecture sketch, evaluation plan, asks.

Document 2

Business Intake Brief

Problem statement, users, desired outcomes, constraints.

Pre-work

Candidate pilot corpus list

Example: “Exec Forum minutes folder + packs folder (last 6 months)”.

Pre-work

Draft success metrics

Time saved baseline plan + citation correctness measurement plan.

AI lead tip: Put the “decisions required” at the top of the invite so people understand it is not a status meeting.

3) Who must attend (and why)

Role Why they must be there What decision they influence
Business Owner Accountable for outcome + adoption. Pilot cohort, success metrics, rollout approach.
Sponsor / GM Authority + unblocker (budget, access, priority). Go/no-go, escalation path, cross-team alignment.
EA representative Defines exec workflow and what “usable output” looks like. Output format, top questions, acceptance criteria.
Ops representative Defines reliability needs and failure tolerance. Safe failure behavior, latency expectations, incident concerns.
Data Owner(s) Approves corpus access and exclusions. What documents are allowed in pilot and how they’re classified.
Security / IAM Confirms approved patterns (identity, access control, secrets, logging). Access model constraints, audit requirements, network controls.
Privacy / Risk Ensures the pilot meets obligations for sensitive data. Redaction/retention rules, allowed processing boundary.
EM (Engineering Manager) Confirms team capacity and delivery plan. Resourcing, sprint cadence, definition of done.
Lead AI Engineer Chair of the meeting. Converts decisions into build plan. Defines evaluation gates, architecture pack, and delivery approach.
Common mistake: leaving out the data owner or EA rep. Result: you build something nobody can approve or nobody finds usable.

4) Agenda (60–90 minutes)

  1. Context recap (5 mins) — problem, pilot intent, why citations and access control are mandatory.
  2. Workflow + output contract (15 mins) — EA validates “what good looks like”.
  3. Pilot scope decisions (20 mins) — cohort, corpus, exclusions, timeline.
  4. Security/privacy gates (15 mins) — confirm constraints and approval path.
  5. Success metrics + evaluation gates (15 mins) — define baseline and go-live criteria.
  6. Next steps & owners (10 mins) — assign people to each dependency.
Chairing tip: Treat this as a decision-making meeting, not brainstorming. If discussion loops, park it and force a decision on the minimum safe pilot.

5) The 3 decisions you must get (non-negotiable)

Decision 1 — Pilot cohort

  • Exactly who gets access (names or an org group)
  • Who is the pilot champion (often an EA manager)

Decision 2 — Pilot corpus (approved document sets)

  • Exact doc locations/sources
  • Explicit exclusions (HR/legal/customer PII-heavy docs)
  • Named data owner approval path

Decision 3 — Success metrics and go-live gate

  • 2–3 measurable metrics (time saved, citation correctness, adoption)
  • Definition of “ready to launch pilot” (minimum eval thresholds)
If you get only these three decisions, you can start delivery. Everything else can iterate, but these prevent rework and approval deadlocks.

6) Questions to ask (by role)

Questions for the Business Owner

  • “What is the one outcome we must prove in 6 weeks?”
  • “Which user cohort will actually use this weekly?”
  • “Who owns change management and onboarding?”

Questions for the Sponsor / GM

  • “If access approvals stall, who can unblock within 48 hours?”
  • “What is the acceptable pilot timeline and what tradeoffs are allowed?”

Questions for the EA representative

  • “What are the top 20 recurring questions execs ask?”
  • “What counts as proof: snippet + link, or snippet + timestamp, or both?”
  • “If the tool says ‘not found’, what should it suggest next?”

Questions for Ops

  • “What happens if the answer is wrong?”
  • “What latency is acceptable in urgent moments?”
  • “What must the tool do when evidence is insufficient?”

Questions for Data Owners

  • “Which folders/sites are approved, and which are explicitly excluded?”
  • “Are there retention rules or classification tags we must enforce?”

Questions for Security/IAM + Privacy/Risk

  • “What is the approved pattern for identity, service accounts, and audit logging?”
  • “What data can be sent to the LLM and what must be redacted/blocked?”
  • “What is the minimum go-live checklist for a pilot?”

7) Outputs you produce after the meeting (within 24–48 hours)

Output A — Decision record (1 page)

  • Pilot cohort (names/group)
  • Pilot corpus + exclusions
  • Success metrics + go-live gate
  • Owners for approvals and dependencies

Output B — Architecture review pack (HLD)

  • System diagram + trust boundaries
  • Identity/access model
  • Data flow (what is stored, what is sent to the LLM)
  • Audit + logging plan
  • Risks + mitigations

Output C — Sprint-ready backlog

  • Epics by lane (ingestion, backend/security, retrieval/LLM/eval)
  • Owners + milestones (vertical slice early)
AI lead tip: If you don’t write a decision record, the organisation will “remember” the meeting differently.

8) Common failure modes (and how to avoid them)

  • Failure: “We’ll decide the corpus later.” → Fix: no corpus, no pilot. Decide now.
  • Failure: “It should search everything.” → Fix: pilot needs boundaries; scale comes later.
  • Failure: “We want it to be smart, no need for citations.” → Fix: enterprise trust requires citations.
  • Failure: No EA/ops voice in the room → Fix: co-design is mandatory.
  • Failure: No owner for approvals → Fix: assign a person per dependency.
What kills pilots fastest: unclear data ownership + unclear access control + unclear success metrics.
