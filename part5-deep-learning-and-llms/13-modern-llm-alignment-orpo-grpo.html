Modern LLM Alignment — Beyond DPO (ORPO, GRPO, KTO, RLAIF)

Where this fits Chapter 12 covered SFT (supervised fine‑tuning) and PEFT (LoRA / QLoRA) — how to make a base LLM follow your instructions and your domain. This chapter is about the next step: preference alignment — how to make the model prefer good outputs over bad outputs at scale, without the fragility of full PPO‑style RLHF.

1) The lineage in one paragraph

RLHF (Christiano 2017, InstructGPT 2022) trains a reward model from human preference pairs, then uses PPO to optimize the LLM against that reward while a KL term tethers it to the SFT model. It works, but it requires three models in memory (policy, reference, reward) and PPO hyper‑parameter tuning. DPO (Rafailov 2023) showed you can skip the explicit reward model and PPO and instead minimize a closed‑form classification loss directly on preference pairs. The 2024–2025 wave — KTO, ORPO, GRPO, RLAIF — refines this further along three axes: data shape, memory cost, and signal source.

2) DPO recap (the baseline)

DPO assumes a Bradley–Terry preference model and trains directly on triples $(x, y_w, y_l)$ where $y_w$ is the chosen response and $y_l$ is the rejected one. The loss is:

$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{DPO}} = -\log\sigma\!\left( \beta \log\dfrac{\pi_\theta(y_w|x)}{\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(y_w|x)} - \beta \log\dfrac{\pi_\theta(y_l|x)}{\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(y_l|x)} \right)$

Two models in memory (policy + frozen reference), $\beta$ controls how far the policy can drift from the reference. Pros: simple, stable. Cons: needs a strong SFT model to start from, and needs paired preference data.

3) ORPO — Odds‑Ratio Preference Optimization (Hong et al., 2024)

ORPO removes the reference model entirely and merges SFT and preference optimization into a single loss:

$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ORPO}} = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{SFT}}(y_w \mid x) + \lambda \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{OR}}$

where $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{OR}}$ is the negative log‑sigmoid of the log odds‑ratio between the chosen and rejected responses:

$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{OR}} = -\log\sigma\!\left(\log\dfrac{\mathrm{odds}_\theta(y_w|x)}{\mathrm{odds}_\theta(y_l|x)}\right), \quad \mathrm{odds}_\theta(y|x) = \dfrac{\pi_\theta(y|x)}{1-\pi_\theta(y|x)}$

Why people like it:

  • One model in memory — no frozen reference policy. ~50 % less VRAM than DPO.
  • You can go from a base model directly to an aligned model in a single training run; no separate SFT phase needed.
  • Empirically competitive with DPO on Llama / Mistral 7B benchmarks.

Use when: you have paired preference data and want the simplest, cheapest pipeline. The TRL library exposes ORPOTrainer with the same interface as DPOTrainer.

4) KTO — Kahneman–Tversky Optimization (Ethayarajh et al., 2024)

KTO removes the requirement for paired preference data. Instead of $(x, y_w, y_l)$ triples, you label each $(x, y)$ as simply desirable or undesirable — the kind of data you can collect at scale from thumbs‑up / thumbs‑down telemetry.

The loss is shaped after Kahneman–Tversky prospect theory: gains on desirable samples and losses on undesirable samples are weighted asymmetrically (loss aversion). Practically, KTO works well in real product settings where almost no one writes paired comparisons but everyone clicks 👍 / 👎.

Use when: your feedback is binary, unpaired, and noisy (production telemetry, customer ratings).

5) GRPO — Group Relative Policy Optimization (DeepSeek, 2024–2025)

GRPO is the algorithm behind DeepSeek‑R1's reasoning training. It is closer to PPO than to DPO in spirit but cuts the value/critic network entirely.

For each prompt $x$ you sample a group of $G$ responses $\{y_1, \dots, y_G\}$ from the current policy. Each is scored by a reward function $r_i$ (which can be a learned reward model, but in practice is often a verifiable signal: unit‑test pass/fail, math answer match, format check). Compute the advantage as the in‑group standardization:

$A_i = \dfrac{r_i - \mathrm{mean}(r_1,\dots,r_G)}{\mathrm{std}(r_1,\dots,r_G)}$

Then optimize a PPO‑style clipped objective with a KL penalty against the reference policy, using $A_i$ as the per‑sample advantage. No critic network, so memory and stability are much better than PPO.

Why it matters:

  • Uses verifiable rewards (RLVR). For math and code you don't need a reward model at all — graders are deterministic.
  • The “group baseline” replaces the value network with cheap Monte‑Carlo variance reduction.
  • This is what produced DeepSeek‑R1's chain‑of‑thought reasoning gains starting from a base model with essentially no preference labels.

Use when: your task has a programmatic grader (math, code, JSON‑schema compliance, tool‑use success) — i.e. RLVR settings.

6) RLAIF and Constitutional AI

RLAIF (RL from AI Feedback) replaces the human labelers with a strong LLM judge. You generate response pairs, ask a judge model (GPT‑4‑class, Claude, etc.) to pick the better one against a written rubric (the “constitution”), and then run DPO/ORPO/PPO on those AI preferences.

This is now the standard way to scale alignment to millions of samples. Combine it with active learning (only ask the judge when the policy is uncertain) and you have a tight, cheap loop.

8) A practical recipe for an enterprise project

  1. SFT on your domain demonstrations (LoRA / QLoRA — Ch. 12).
  2. Collect production thumbs‑up / thumbs‑down from your pilot (Part I, Ch. 11) — this becomes your KTO training set.
  3. For tasks with verifiable graders (data extraction, JSON output, code, SQL correctness), spin up a small GRPO loop. The grader is typically < 100 lines of Python.
  4. For everything else, run RLAIF → ORPO with a written rubric reviewed by your domain experts. ORPO over DPO purely for the VRAM and pipeline simplicity.
  5. Re‑measure on the eval suite from your AI lead response document (Part I, Ch. 3). Ship only if the metric you committed to moves.
Common pitfall Alignment methods reward what you measure. If your reward model — or your verifiable grader — has a loophole, the policy will find it (reward hacking). Always hold out an evaluation set and inspect samples by hand every iteration.
